Model M Keyboard Restoration

Dec. 2021
Technologies
Soldering, Microcontrollers
Type
Retro computer restoration
Last updated
Nov. 2022
As of writing I have restored three separate IBM keyboard. Two of which are proper Model M keyboards, one blue badge and one grey oval badge. The other keyboard is an M122 Battlecruiser. The M122 is the first IBM keyboard I ever found, used, and have restored. Found in the dusty server room equipment locker of my employer at the time I found it discarded in a pile of unused parts. My M122 was once used as the terminal access keyboard of the AS/400 mainframe that the company used as its database. As a quick side note the company still uses an AS/400 running the exact same data base management software as of writing in July 2023, albeit through an emulator.

When I found my M122 it was not in good shape, and I asked my boss if I could have it since it clearly hasn't been used in years. He agreed and I began to research how to restore such a keyboard. The first step was a cleaning process since it was absolutely disgusting when I found it. The keyboard was covered in ancient dust and grime, covered in marker and pen ink, and missing key caps. Another issues I would face is that the keyboard uses an 8P8C connector.

In order to clean the keyboard, I first needed to disassemble it. This could only be done once I took out the screws. Sadly, I did not have a compatible driver, nor did any hardware store I could find. To take off the back panel I would need a hex driver, but I was not sure which one driver. I was able to borrow a kit from my other place of employment and through trial and error I discovered I needed a 5.5 mm hex driver. Upon first disassembly of the keyboard, I was finally able to find a part number on the birth certificate of the keyboard. This M122 was made 28. August 1998 by IBM in the UK. It is P/N 1394167 but really has an Assembly Part Number of 1394177. I put the keyboard back together and returned the driver to my place of work the following day. Using the new identifying information, I had now found I began more researching and was able to put together a plan on how to clean and restore the keyboard to working order. The first step was to order a precision driver kit which I did. Now I was able to open the keyboard whenever I needed.
